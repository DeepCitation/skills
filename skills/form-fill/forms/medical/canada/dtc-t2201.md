# Disability Tax Credit Certificate (T2201) — Form Spec

The T2201 is a federal form submitted to the CRA to certify eligibility for the Disability Tax Credit (DTC). Part A is completed by the individual (or their legal representative); Part B is completed by a qualified medical practitioner. The CRA adjudicator uses this form to determine whether the applicant has a "severe and prolonged impairment in physical or mental functions" that meets the legislated standard.

**Reference**: CRA Guide RC4064 (Disability-Related Information)

## Eligibility — the DTC test

The applicant must meet **one** of two pathways — all three conditions must be satisfied within the chosen pathway:

1. **Markedly restricted** — all or substantially all of the time (≥90%), the individual is unable to perform, or takes an inordinate amount of time to perform, at least one basic activity of daily living, even with therapy, appropriate devices, and medication; **OR**
2. **Cumulative effect** — the individual is significantly limited in two or more basic activities of daily living, and the combined effect is equivalent in impact to a single marked restriction.

Additionally, for either pathway:

3. **Prolonged** — the impairment has lasted, or is expected to last, for a continuous period of at least 12 months.

**Basic activities of daily living** covered by the form:
- Vision
- Speaking
- Hearing
- Walking
- Eliminating (bowel or bladder functions)
- Feeding
- Dressing
- Mental functions necessary for everyday life

If the evidence supports marked restriction in any single category, state it explicitly using the CRA's language. Vague language such as "the patient has difficulty walking" is insufficient — the adjudicator requires a specific, quantified account of how the impairment meets the ≥90% threshold.

## Section 1: Patient Information (Part A)

**Fields**: Full name, SIN, date of birth, mailing address, phone number; name and contact of legal representative (if applicable)

- **Source**: Patient-provided demographics; legal documentation if a representative is completing the form
- **Citations**: Not required for patient-provided demographic data
- **Pitfall**: SIN must match CRA records exactly — a transposed digit causes processing delays. Confirm DOB against a government-issued ID if the file contains conflicting records.
- **Note**: Part A is completed by the individual or their legal representative, not the practitioner. The agent's role is to ensure the personal information section is consistent with the documents provided before passing Part B to the practitioner for completion.

## Section 2: Effects of Impairment (Part B)

This is the core of the form. The practitioner must describe **how** the impairment restricts each applicable activity — not merely that it does. The CRA adjudicator is looking for functional description, not diagnostic labels.

For each activity category below, the practitioner answers whether the patient is markedly restricted, and must provide supporting details.

### 2a. Vision

**Fields**: Visual acuity, field of vision, whether the patient is markedly restricted in seeing even with corrective lenses

- **Source**: Ophthalmologist or optometrist reports, visual acuity measurements (Snellen chart or equivalent), visual field testing results
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - Specific acuity measurements (e.g., "best corrected visual acuity of 20/400 in the better eye")
  - Visual field loss quantified in degrees or percentage
  - Statement that restriction persists even with the best corrective lenses or devices available
  - Whether the restriction is present all or substantially all of the time
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Stating "low vision" without providing acuity measurements
  - Not addressing whether correction (glasses, contacts) was applied
  - Describing a fluctuating condition without stating the proportion of time the patient is restricted
- **Terminology**: "Markedly restricted in seeing" — the CRA's standard is the inability to see well enough to perform the task all or substantially all of the time, even with corrective lenses

### 2b. Speaking

**Fields**: Ability to be understood by a familiar person in a quiet setting, whether the patient is markedly restricted

- **Source**: Speech-language pathologist assessments, ENT or neurologist reports, clinical notes documenting communication ability
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - Specific description of speech intelligibility (e.g., "patient is understood less than 25% of the time by a familiar listener in a quiet environment")
  - Reference to standardized intelligibility assessments if available
  - Whether electronic communication aids are used and whether restriction persists with aids
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Conflating speaking ability with language comprehension — the DTC speaking category is about producing intelligible speech, not receptive language
  - Not addressing the "familiar person, quiet setting" standard (the CRA's benchmark for this category)
- **Terminology**: "Markedly restricted in speaking" — unable to be understood by a familiar person in a quiet place all or substantially all of the time

### 2c. Hearing

**Fields**: Audiometric results, ability to understand spoken conversation in a quiet setting even with a hearing device

- **Source**: Audiologist reports, audiogram results (pure-tone averages, speech discrimination scores), ENT reports
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - Pure-tone average (PTA) in the better ear — typically 90 dB HL or worse supports marked restriction
  - Speech discrimination score even with optimal hearing device in place
  - Whether restriction persists with the best available hearing aid or cochlear implant
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Submitting only an audiogram without accompanying speech discrimination data
  - Not addressing hearing device use — if the patient uses a hearing aid, the adjudicator needs to know whether restriction persists with the device in place
  - Confusing hearing threshold with functional hearing ability in conversation
- **Terminology**: "Markedly restricted in hearing" — unable to understand oral communication all or substantially all of the time even with a hearing device

### 2d. Walking

**Fields**: Ability to walk on level ground, distance walked before stopping due to the impairment, use of assistive devices

- **Source**: Physiotherapy or occupational therapy functional assessments, specialist reports (orthopedics, neurology, respirology, cardiology), clinical notes documenting gait and ambulation
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - **Specific distance** — "patient cannot walk more than 50 meters on level ground without stopping due to pain/breathlessness/fatigue" is actionable; "patient has difficulty walking" is not
  - Whether assistive devices are used (cane, walker, wheelchair) and whether restriction persists with them
  - The underlying cause linked explicitly to the functional limitation (e.g., "severe COPD limiting ambulation to 30 meters before requiring rest due to dyspnea")
  - Whether the restriction is present all or substantially all of the time (not just on bad days)
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Describing the diagnosis without the functional walking limit ("patient has peripheral artery disease" gives the adjudicator nothing to act on)
  - Not specifying "level ground" — the DTC standard assumes flat terrain; hills or stairs are not the benchmark
  - Omitting device use, leaving the adjudicator to assume the restriction might be resolved with a device
- **Terminology**: "Markedly restricted in walking" — takes an inordinate amount of time to walk, even with appropriate therapy, medication, and devices

### 2e. Eliminating (bowel or bladder functions)

**Fields**: Ability to control bowel and/or bladder function, frequency of accidents, devices or therapies used (catheters, ostomy)

- **Source**: Gastroenterology or urology reports, clinical notes documenting frequency and severity of incontinence or retention, continence management plans
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - Frequency and predictability of accidents (e.g., "patient experiences urinary incontinence multiple times daily despite maximum tolerated pharmacotherapy")
  - Time burden of continence management (e.g., "intermittent catheterization required 6 times daily, each episode taking 20–30 minutes")
  - Whether restriction persists with optimal management devices and medications
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Understating the time burden — the CRA includes time spent on bowel/bladder management routines when assessing "inordinate amount of time"
  - Not mentioning devices (catheters, ostomy bags) and their management requirements
- **Terminology**: "Markedly restricted in eliminating" — the time spent on managing elimination functions must be included in the time calculation

### 2f. Feeding

**Fields**: Ability to feed oneself (not to prepare food — only the act of getting food from a plate into the mouth), use of adaptive equipment

- **Source**: Occupational therapy assessments, neurologist or physiatrist reports, clinical notes documenting feeding ability
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - Specific description of what the patient cannot do (e.g., "patient cannot bring utensils to mouth due to severe hand tremor; requires another person to feed")
  - Whether adaptive utensils are used and whether restriction persists with them
  - Time burden if the patient can self-feed but takes an inordinate amount of time
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Confusing feeding (the act of self-feeding) with food preparation — meal preparation is **not** a DTC feeding category; only the act of getting food into the mouth counts
  - Omitting tube feeding requirements and their time burden if applicable
- **Terminology**: "Markedly restricted in feeding oneself" — the DTC feeding category is strictly the physical act of self-feeding, not cooking or meal planning

### 2g. Dressing

**Fields**: Ability to put on and take off clothing appropriate to conditions, use of adaptive aids

- **Source**: Occupational therapy assessments, physiotherapy reports, clinical notes documenting upper or lower limb function
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - What specifically the patient cannot manage (e.g., "patient cannot button garments, manage zippers, or tie footwear due to bilateral hand weakness; requires assistance to dress and undress")
  - Whether adaptive clothing or aids are used and whether restriction persists
  - Time burden for patients who can dress but take an inordinate amount of time
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Only addressing partial dressing difficulty — the adjudicator expects a complete picture (upper and lower body, footwear)
  - Not noting whether restriction is present all or substantially all of the time vs. only during flares
- **Terminology**: "Markedly restricted in dressing oneself" — includes putting on and taking off weather-appropriate clothing

### 2h. Mental Functions Necessary for Everyday Life

**Fields**: Adaptive functioning (memory, problem-solving, goal-setting, judgment), whether the patient requires significant assistance or supervision

- **Source**: Neuropsychological assessments, psychiatry reports, occupational therapy cognitive assessments, clinical notes documenting cognitive functioning; validated instruments (e.g., MoCA, WHODAS 2.0, GAF)
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - **Adaptive functioning** — memory for daily tasks (taking medications, keeping appointments), problem-solving in everyday situations, ability to plan and manage finances, judgment and safety awareness
  - **Supervision needs** — does the patient require someone else to prompt, remind, or oversee basic daily tasks?
  - **Validated scores** — quantified measures (GAF, MoCA, WHODAS) with numeric values carry more weight than clinical impressions alone
  - Specific examples of failure in everyday functions (e.g., "patient forgets to take prescribed medications without daily reminders; has twice left the stove on unattended; cannot manage bank transactions independently")
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Using clinical terms without functional translation ("patient has significant cognitive impairment" — the adjudicator needs to know *what tasks* the patient cannot perform)
  - Confusing mental health diagnosis with functional restriction — a diagnosis of depression does not automatically qualify; the adjudicator needs to see how the symptoms translate into daily functional failure
  - Not addressing the "all or substantially all of the time" criterion — fluctuating conditions require a statement of the proportion of time the restriction applies
- **Terminology**: "Markedly restricted in mental functions necessary for everyday life" — this category covers adaptive functioning, not intelligence. Focus on what the patient cannot reliably do on their own in daily life.

## Section 3: Diagnosis

**Fields**: Primary diagnosis, relevant secondary diagnoses, ICD code if available

- **Source**: Clinical notes, specialist reports, hospital discharge summaries
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - Specific diagnostic terminology (ICD-10 codes strengthen precision)
  - Diagnoses that directly link to the restricted activities described in Section 2
  - Secondary diagnoses that compound the primary condition's functional impact
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - **Diagnosis alone does not qualify** — the single most common cause of DTC rejection is a form that reads like a diagnosis letter rather than a functional assessment. The adjudicator must connect "this diagnosis" to "these specific functional restrictions" to "this activity category"
  - Using layperson terms ("bad back", "nerve damage") instead of clinical diagnoses
  - Omitting comorbidities that strengthen the functional picture
- **Terminology**: After listing the diagnosis, explicitly bridge it to function: "This diagnosis results in [specific functional restriction] that meets the criterion for marked restriction in [activity category]."

## Section 4: Duration

**Fields**: Date impairment began (or is expected to begin), whether the impairment is expected to last at least 12 continuous months, whether it is permanent

- **Source**: Clinical notes with earliest documented onset, specialist reports with prognosis statements, imaging or lab results supporting permanence
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - **Clear onset date** consistent across all source records
  - An explicit statement that the impairment has lasted or is expected to last 12+ continuous months
  - For permanent conditions: "the impairment is permanent and is not expected to improve with further treatment"
  - For degenerative conditions: note the trajectory ("expected to worsen over time, not improve")
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Hedging language that undermines the duration criterion ("may persist for 12 months" — state it affirmatively if the evidence supports it)
  - Inconsistent onset dates across sections — the adjudicator will compare the onset date in Section 4 against the diagnosis history in Section 3
  - Forgetting that "prolonged" means a **continuous** 12-month period — if the impairment is episodic, the practitioner must address whether the underlying condition causing restriction is itself continuous even if episodes vary in severity
- **Terminology**: "The impairment is severe and prolonged" — use the CRA's exact phrase when supported by the evidence. Avoid "stable" in isolation, as it may be interpreted as "manageable" rather than "permanent."

---

## Section 5: Life-Sustaining Therapy

**Fields**: Whether the patient requires life-sustaining therapy; description of the therapy; frequency per week; average hours per week (including travel to/from therapy and recovery time)

- **Source**: Treatment records, pharmacy records, dialysis or therapy logs, clinical notes documenting therapy schedule
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - **Frequency threshold**: therapy must be required at least 3 times per week
  - **Time threshold**: must average at least 14 hours per week — CRA explicitly includes travel time to/from the therapy location and recovery time following the session
  - Examples include: kidney dialysis, postural drainage for cystic fibrosis, insulin therapy for type 1 diabetes (with associated monitoring tasks), respiratory therapy
  - The therapy must be essential to sustaining vital functions — it cannot be general wellness or preventive care
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Understating the weekly time burden by omitting travel and recovery — the 14-hour threshold is frequently met only when these are included
  - Describing therapy that is beneficial but not life-sustaining (e.g., physiotherapy for mobility is not life-sustaining therapy under the DTC)
  - Not documenting that the therapy is ongoing and expected to continue for 12+ months
- **Terminology**: "Life-sustaining therapy" — must be essential to sustain a vital function. State explicitly: "This therapy is required to sustain [vital function] and without it the patient's life would be at risk."
- **Note**: A patient who qualifies under life-sustaining therapy may still qualify even if no single activity of daily living category is markedly restricted.

---

## Section 6: Cumulative Effect

**Fields**: Two or more activity categories in which the patient is significantly (but not necessarily markedly) limited; combined impact assessment

- **Source**: Same sources as Section 2 — the cumulative effect pathway draws on multiple categories, so the documentation must cover each
- **What the adjudicator looks for**:
  - At least two categories where the patient is "significantly limited" (not necessarily at the ≥90% threshold, but still a meaningful functional restriction)
  - An explicit statement that the combined effect of the limitations is equivalent in impact to a single marked restriction
  - Specific functional description for each category — the same rules apply as Section 2: describe *how* the limitation manifests, not just that it exists
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Listing two categories of mild limitation and expecting the cumulative pathway to apply — "significantly limited" still requires a meaningful restriction in each category, not a trivial inconvenience
  - Not explicitly stating that the combined effect is equivalent to a marked restriction — the adjudicator needs this bridge statement
  - Failing to document each category individually — a single paragraph covering both categories rarely provides enough detail
- **Terminology**: "Significantly limited" — the CRA uses this term specifically for the cumulative pathway. Do not use "markedly restricted" unless a single category meets the ≥90% threshold. Both can appear in the same form if appropriate.

| Duration | Earliest clinical note documenting condition, specialist prognosis letters | Medication records showing onset, disability insurer records |
| Life-Sustaining Therapy | Therapy logs, pharmacy records, dialysis records | Treatment plans, referral letters |
| Cumulative Effect | All specialist reports covering each restricted category | Functional capacity evaluations, OT assessments |

## Common Rejection Reasons

1. **Diagnosis described instead of functional effects** — "patient has MS" tells the adjudicator nothing; "patient cannot walk more than 20 meters on level ground without stopping due to fatigue and spasticity" does
2. **"All or substantially all of the time" not addressed** — the adjudicator needs to see that the restriction meets the ≥90% threshold, not just that it occurs sometimes
3. **Vague functional language** — "difficulty performing daily activities" is rejected; "requires 45 minutes to dress due to bilateral hand weakness, compared to approximately 5 minutes for a person of similar age without impairment" is accepted
4. **Duration requirement missing** — the form must explicitly address the 12-month continuous duration; an otherwise complete form can be rejected solely for this omission
5. **Therapy/medication not linked to ongoing limitation** — if the patient's impairment is substantially controlled by medication or devices, the practitioner must still address whether a marked restriction persists despite that treatment
6. **Cumulative effect pathway used without sufficient per-category detail** — each category claimed under cumulative effect must be individually documented with the same rigor as a single-category claim
7. **Practitioner specialty mismatch** — CRA requires the certifying practitioner to be qualified to assess the relevant impairment (e.g., an optometrist for vision, an audiologist or physician for hearing). Confirm the certifying practitioner's scope before submitting.

## Canada-Specific Notes

- The DTC is a non-refundable federal tax credit — it reduces federal income tax payable. If the applicant does not pay sufficient federal tax to benefit, the credit may be transferred to a supporting family member.
- **Retroactive claims**: The DTC can be claimed retroactively for up to 10 years (subject to normal tax adjustment rules). When documenting onset, confirm the earliest year the impairment met the eligibility standard.
- **Registered Disability Savings Plan (RDSP)**: DTC eligibility is a prerequisite for opening an RDSP — note this when the patient or family asks about long-term financial planning.
- **Child Disability Benefit**: DTC-eligible children may qualify for the Child Disability Benefit — a separate payment administered through the Canada Child Benefit.
- **Provincial supplements**: Some provinces offer additional credits or benefits tied to DTC eligibility (e.g., Ontario's Ontario Disability Support Program has a different test, but DTC approval is sometimes used as supporting evidence). Do not conflate provincial programs with the federal DTC.
- CRA may request additional information or reassess — a well-documented T2201 with specific functional language reduces the likelihood of a reassessment request.
- The form is signed by a **qualified medical practitioner** — the eligible practitioner types depend on the category of impairment (e.g., a medical doctor can certify any category; an optometrist can certify vision; an audiologist can certify hearing). Confirm the certifying practitioner meets CRA's requirements for the relevant category before submitting.
