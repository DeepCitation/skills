# Short-Term Disability Physician's Statement — Form Spec

The STD Physician's Statement supports a claim for short-term disability benefits, typically covering the first 15–26 weeks of disability (varies by policy). STD forms focus on acute conditions, expected recovery timelines, and return-to-work planning — the time horizon is weeks to months, not years.

## Key Differences from LTD

- **Shorter time horizon** — STD covers weeks to months; prognosis should project within this window
- **"Own occupation" test only** — STD always measures against the patient's current job duties
- **Recovery emphasis** — the insurer expects most STD claims to resolve. Frame answers around recovery trajectory, not permanence
- **Elimination period** — typically 0–14 days (shorter than LTD's 90–180 days)
- **Transition to LTD** — if the condition won't resolve within the STD benefit period, note that LTD application may be warranted

## Section 1: Patient and Employment Information

**Fields**: Patient name, DOB, employer, policy/group number, occupation/job title, date last worked, expected return date

- **Source**: Patient-provided information
- **Citations**: Not required for demographic/employment data
- **Important**: "Date last worked" and "expected return date" are the two most scrutinized fields. Be as specific as possible with dates.
- **Pitfall**: An open-ended "unknown return date" on an STD form signals that this should be an LTD claim instead

## Section 2: Diagnosis and Onset

**Fields**: Primary diagnosis with ICD-10 code, secondary diagnoses, date of onset, date of first consultation, is the condition work-related, is this a recurrence of a prior condition

- **Source**: Clinical notes, emergency/urgent care records, specialist reports
- **What the insurer reviewer looks for**:
  - Acute onset or acute exacerbation of a chronic condition
  - Temporal alignment — onset date should match or closely precede the date last worked
  - Whether this is a recurrence (may affect benefit eligibility under pre-existing condition clauses)
  - Objective findings from the initial assessment
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Chronic conditions without an acute event — STD is harder to justify for gradual deterioration (frame as "acute exacerbation" if the evidence supports it)
  - Missing the recurrence question — insurers flag omissions

## Section 3: Current Treatment

**Fields**: Current medications with dosages, therapies in progress, surgical procedures (completed or scheduled), referrals pending

- **Source**: Medication lists, therapy records, surgical scheduling, referral letters
- **What the insurer reviewer looks for**:
  - Active treatment appropriate to the diagnosis
  - Timely specialist referrals (delays in referral suggest the condition may not warrant time off work)
  - Scheduled procedures with dates — these anchor the expected recovery timeline
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - "Watchful waiting" without active treatment — the insurer may question whether the condition is truly disabling
  - Missing surgical dates when surgery is the recovery trigger

## Section 4: Functional Limitations

**Fields**: Physical limitations, cognitive limitations, specific job duties the patient cannot perform, can the patient perform modified duties

- **Source**: Clinical examination findings, specialist assessments, functional capacity data
- **What the insurer reviewer looks for**:
  - Limitations tied to the acute condition (not pre-existing baseline limitations)
  - Specific job duties affected — "patient is a nurse and cannot perform patient transfers due to lumbar disc herniation" is far stronger than "limited lifting"
  - Modified duty potential — can the patient do desk work, part-time hours, or restricted duties? The insurer will push for partial return to work if medically feasible
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Describing chronic baseline limitations rather than the acute change
  - Not addressing modified duty capability (silence is read as "hasn't considered it")
- **Citation density**: High — each limitation needs a clinical source

## Section 5: Prognosis and Return to Work

**Fields**: Expected recovery timeline, anticipated return-to-work date (full duties), can the patient return to modified duties earlier, barriers to recovery, follow-up plan

- **Source**: Clinical trajectory notes, specialist opinions, surgical recovery guidelines, treatment response data
- **What the insurer reviewer looks for**:
  - **Specific dates** — "expected to return to full duties by [date]" or "anticipated 6–8 week recovery following surgery on [date]"
  - **Graduated return** — a phased return plan (e.g., "modified duties starting week 4, full duties by week 8") is viewed favorably
  - **Objective recovery markers** — what will signal readiness to return? (e.g., "pain-free range of motion", "PHQ-9 score below 10", "cleared by surgeon post-op")
  - **Transition to LTD** — if recovery is expected to exceed the STD benefit period, state this clearly
- **Common pitfalls**:
  - Vague timelines ("a few weeks", "when feeling better") — be specific even if approximate
  - Not addressing graduated return options
  - Failing to flag when the condition may exceed STD duration
- **Terminology**: Use "temporary total disability" when the patient cannot work at all during recovery; "temporary partial disability" when modified duties are possible

## Section 6: Physician Declaration

**Fields**: Physician name, clinic, specialty, CPSA registration, date of last examination, next appointment date, signature

- **Source**: Signing physician details from intake checklist
- **Important**: "Next appointment date" matters for STD — it shows active follow-up and monitoring
- **Note**: Draft but mark as requiring physician review and signature

## STD-Specific Considerations

- **Surgical claims**: If the STD is for a scheduled surgery, the form should reference the pre-operative diagnosis, the procedure, and the expected post-operative recovery timeline. Surgeons' standard recovery guidelines (e.g., "6 weeks post-arthroscopic knee surgery") strengthen the claim.
- **Mental health claims**: STD for mental health conditions requires objective measures (PHQ-9, GAF, WHODAS) — not just the patient's self-report. Document the severity score and the threshold that indicates functional impairment.
- **Pregnancy/maternity**: Pregnancy-related STD (e.g., complications requiring bed rest) uses the same form but the insurer may have specific supplementary requirements. Note if applicable.
- **Recurrent claims**: If the patient has had prior STD claims for the same condition, document what changed (new surgery, acute exacerbation, treatment failure) to distinguish this episode.
